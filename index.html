Working theory
Core question: can U.S./Israeli strikes suppress meaningful missile and drone launch capability before political and economic costs force them to back off? If yes, the regime becomes much more vulnerable. This dashboard is built to test that theory with daily tempo, target spread, oil stress, and business risk — not cumulative scoreboards.

          Business impact — Iranfarhang

          Books, periodicals, library services, and informational-materials logistics through Tehran → Atlanta → end customer.
          • Main vulnerability: freight continuity, customs friction, client procurement hesitation, and payment workflow complexity — not the basic legal concept of informational-materials trade.
          • Best case: sourcing remains possible, transit slows but continues, and institutional buyers tolerate delay if compliance language stays conservative.
          • Most likely: more shipment irregularity, longer cycle times, higher freight cost, tighter compliance questions from universities/libraries, and heavier dependence on the Atlanta node as a trust anchor.
          • Worst realistic case: air-export disruption from Iran or payment friction around third-country invoicing causes procurement delays, missed subscription cycles, and reputational strain with institutional buyers.

          Business impact — Iran/UAE import-export corridor

          Iran-linked trade using Dubai/UAE for settlement, re-export, transshipment, and shipping access.
          • Main vulnerability: banking de-risking and payment friction first, logistics disruption second, direct asset seizure a distant third.
          • Best case: Dubai keeps functioning as a pressure-release valve, but with more scrutiny, slower transfers, and selective compliance tightening.
          • Most likely: more transaction monitoring, more exchange-house friction, higher documentation burden, shipping rerouting, and episodic cargo delay rather than full shutdown.
          • Worst realistic case: broad banking disengagement from Iran-linked clients, restrictions on exchange-house activity, and Gulf shipping interruption severe enough to break working-capital cycles.
